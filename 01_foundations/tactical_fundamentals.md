# TACTICAL FUNDAMENTALS
## Doctrine-Grounded Combat Principles for WCS Conflict

> **Doctrine basis:** ATP 3-21.8 (Infantry Platoon and Squad), FM 3-21.8
> **Application:** WCS NATO/RUS Conflict servers, all maps

---

## THE FOUNDATION: FIRE AND MOVEMENT

Every tactical action in Arma Reforger — and in real infantry combat — rests on a single principle:

> *"Tactical maneuver is fire plus movement. Fire without movement is indecisive. Exposed movement without fire is potentially disastrous."*
> — ATP 3-21.8

This is not a slogan. It is the mechanical description of how small units defeat enemies and survive. When you are moving across open ground without anyone covering you, you are exposed and potentially dead. When you are firing without advancing, you are suppressing but not winning. The combination — one element suppresses while another moves — is how you take ground, break contact, and destroy the enemy.

Every formation, every SOP, every tactic in this library is an application of this principle to a specific situation.

## METT-TC: THE DECISION FRAMEWORK

Before any tactical decision — where to push, when to assault, how to move — military leaders use METT-TC to analyze the situation. In WCS Conflict, this translates directly:

| Factor | Real Meaning | In WCS |
|---|---|---|
| **M** — Mission | What are you trying to accomplish? | Current objective — capture, defend, interdict, support |
| **E** — Enemy | What is the enemy doing and capable of? | Where is Russia, what are they building, where are their trucks? |
| **T** — Terrain & Weather | How does the ground favor or hinder you? | Elevation, treelines, roads, day/night cycle |
| **T** — Troops Available | What do you have to work with? | Your squad composition, rank, available vehicles |
| **T** — Time | How much time do you have? | Match timer, supply levels, how fast Russia is building |
| **C** — Civil Considerations | Non-combatants, civilian impact | Less relevant in Conflict mode, but includes neutral AI and FIA behavior |

Running a quick mental METT-TC before a push takes thirty seconds. It prevents the most common mistake in WCS: pushing without understanding what you are walking into.

## OCOKA: READING THE TERRAIN

Terrain analysis uses the OCOKA framework. Use this when selecting any position — FOB location, observation post, ambush site, mortar pit, MG nest.

| Factor | Question to Ask | WCS Application |
|---|---|---|
| **O** — Observation & Fields of Fire | Can you see and shoot from here? Can the enemy see and shoot you? | Does this MG nest cover the approach? Can the OP see the objective? |
| **C** — Cover & Concealment | Does the terrain stop bullets (cover) or just hide you (concealment)? | Treelines conceal. Buildings provide cover. Know the difference. |
| **O** — Obstacles | What blocks movement? | Rivers, cliffs, roads, dragon's teeth, wire — use them to channel the enemy |
| **K** — Key Terrain | What ground, if held, gives decisive advantage? | High ground with observation, bridge crossings, radio tower positions |
| **A** — Avenues of Approach | How will the enemy come at you? | Main roads, ridgelines, valley floors — the likely routes for armor and trucks |

Pucker Point COP exists because a proper OCOKA analysis of the Chernarus terrain identified that grid 085 040 provides observation over all four primary objectives, concealment from all major approach routes, and cover from direct observation in the treeline.

## MOVEMENT FORMATIONS

*Based on ATP 3-21.8 Chapter 3 — adapted for Arma squad mechanics*

Formations serve two purposes: they put firepower where it is needed, and they give every element a known position so battle drills execute automatically without orders.

**Maintain 10–15 meter spacing between players at all times.** This is not optional. One grenade into a clustered squad eliminates multiple players. Distance also breaks up visual signatures and confuses enemy fire.

### The Wedge

```
         [POINT]
        /        \
   [LEFT]        [RIGHT]
      \              /
       [LEFT REAR]  [RIGHT REAR]
              |
           [REAR]
```

**Use for:** Standard patrol in moderate-threat terrain. Provides 270° observation coverage. The point man leads and sets the pace. All others key on the point.

**Strength:** Flexible, easy to transition to online or bounding overwatch from. The base formation for most WCS patrol situations.

### Column

```
[POINT]
   |
[2ND]
   |
[3RD]
   |
[REAR]
```

**Use for:** Movement along roads, through dense vegetation, or any terrain that prevents spreading out. Fastest movement but poorest security.

**Critical note:** Never use column across open ground. This is how entire squads get wiped by a single MG position.

### Line (On-Line)

```
[LEFT] — [CENTER-L] — [CENTER] — [CENTER-R] — [RIGHT]
```

**Use for:** The final assault on an objective or a defensive position. All elements advance or fire simultaneously. Maximum firepower to the front.

**Use when:** You have positive ID on the enemy position and are ready to assault through it. Not for movement.

### Bounding Overwatch

Not a formation but a movement technique — the most important one in the game.

**Element A** establishes a position behind cover and aims down sights covering the route.
**Element B** sprints to the next covered position.
**Element B covers. Element A moves. Repeat.**

This is how you cross open ground without dying. The element providing overwatch is not moving — they are actively watching for threats and ready to fire immediately. The moving element goes fast and goes prone behind cover before pausing.

**In practice:** Even two players can do this effectively. One player covers while the other moves. Switch roles at each bound.

## IMMEDIATE ACTION DRILLS

Immediate action drills are pre-planned responses to specific situations. They must be fast because there is no time to plan under fire. The unit that reacts first controls the situation.

### React to Contact — The 3 Ds

When you take unexpected fire:

**1. DISTANCE** — Return fire immediately, even if you cannot see the enemy. Volume of fire suppresses. Suppression buys time. Do not freeze.

**2. DIRECTION** — Identify where the fire is coming from and call it: *"Contact East, 150 meters."* Your squad needs this to respond.

**3. DESCRIPTION** — Add detail as you have it: *"Two infantry, treeline at the road junction."*

After the initial reaction: seek hard cover (not just concealment), get weapons onto the threat, and transition to a deliberate response — support by fire and flanking assault, or break contact.

> **Doctrine note:** ATP 3-21.8 describes this as "React to Ambush" and "React to Direct Fire Contact." The immediate actions are nearly identical: return fire, take cover, report, control the engagement. The key insight is that the unit that returns fire fastest controls the moment — even suppressing fire that misses prevents the enemy from moving or aiming accurately.

### Break Contact

When the fight is lost or the position is untenable:

1. Designate a withdrawal direction and a rally point
2. One element provides covering fire while the other bounds rearward
3. Alternate until contact is broken
4. Reassemble at the rally point and reassess

**Never run in a straight line away from contact.** You will be shot in the back. Bound rearward using cover.

## SUPPORT BY FIRE — THE CORE OFFENSIVE TACTIC

Support by fire (SBF) is how small units defeat larger defending forces. It is the direct application of fire and movement to the offense.

**Step 1 — Identify the enemy position.** Recon or observation establishes where the enemy is and how they are oriented.

**Step 2 — Establish a Support by Fire position.** An element with MGs, automatic weapons, or volume of fire moves to a position that can engage the enemy position without being in the enemy's primary field of fire. Ideally on the flank.

**Step 3 — Initiate suppression.** The SBF element opens fire and pins the enemy — keeping their heads down, preventing them from moving or aiming.

**Step 4 — Assault element flanks.** While suppression is maintained, the assault element uses dead ground to move to a position on the enemy's flank or rear, then assaults through.

**Step 5 — Lift or shift fire.** As the assault element closes to danger close distance, the SBF element shifts fire to prevent fratricide — moves it to secondary positions, lifts it entirely, or shifts to covering enemy escape routes.

**Communication is everything.** The assault element calls when they are ready to move, when they are at danger close, and when the position is clear. Without this, the SBF element cannot lift fire safely.

## THE L-SHAPE AMBUSH

The L-shape ambush is specifically designed to destroy vehicles and logistic convoys — the most valuable targets on a WCS server.

```
                    ROAD
         ←  ←  ←  TRUCK  →  →  →
         
[KILL ELEMENT] ← long leg along road axis
         
         [CUT-OFF ELEMENT] ← short leg perpendicular at far end of kill zone
```

**Kill Element** (long leg): Positioned parallel to the road. Engages the vehicle broadside. Maximum target exposure. AT weapons, MGs, rifles.

**Cut-off Element** (short leg): Positioned at the far end of the kill zone, perpendicular to the road. Engages the front of the vehicle if it tries to drive through, and cuts off any crew that dismounts and runs forward.

**Execution:** All elements hold fire until the vehicle is fully inside the kill zone. Initiation is on the signal of the ambush commander or on the first anti-tank shot. All elements fire simultaneously. The vehicle never reaches the end of the kill zone.

> **Doctrine note:** FM 3-21.8 describes L-shape, linear, and point ambushes. The L-shape is used specifically when the objective is destruction of vehicles and denial of escape. The right angle of the L creates interlocking fields of fire and removes the vehicle's ability to drive through or reverse out of the kill zone.

## DEFENSIVE FUNDAMENTALS

When defending a position — FOB, captured objective, or COP — the same principles apply in reverse:

**Interlocking fields of fire.** Every MG nest and fighting position should cover the dead ground of adjacent positions. No approach should be unobserved and uncovered. Gaps in fields of fire are avenues of approach for the enemy.

**Cover the obstacles.** Barbed wire, dragon's teeth, and roadblocks only work if they are covered by fire. An obstacle the enemy can breach at leisure while you are 300m away is not a tactical obstacle — it is a minor inconvenience. Build obstacles where your weapons can reach them.

**Depth.** Do not put all defensive strength on the outer perimeter. Attackers who penetrate the perimeter should encounter a second layer of resistance. At Pucker Point, MG nests on the outer terrain form the first layer; bunkers around the MCU and mortar pits form the second.

**Know your withdrawal route.** Every defender should know where to go if the position is overrun. A fighting withdrawal to a rally point is not defeat — it is a controlled tactical action. Running without a plan is what gets squads wiped.

## THE FIVE FUNDAMENTALS OF EVERY SOLDIER

ATP 3-21.8 identifies five fundamental skills every infantry soldier must master. In WCS, these translate directly:

| Fundamental | Real | WCS Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Shoot** | Accurate, controlled fire on identified targets | Know your zero, use LRF for range, fire from stable positions |
| **Move** | Tactical movement using cover and formations | Use bounding overwatch, never cross open ground upright without covering fire |
| **Communicate** | Information up, down, and across the chain | Contact reports, fire mission calls, casualty reports — use brevity codes |
| **Survive** | Protect yourself and your equipment | IFAK always carried, position selection, noise and light discipline |
| **Sustain** | Maintain combat capability | Supply runs, resupply at FOBs, weight management for stamina |

A player who executes all five consistently is more valuable to the team than someone who only shoots well.

## CONTACT REPORTING — SALUTE AND TAX

Standard reporting formats used any time you observe enemy forces. Every player who spots the enemy should be able to pass a SALUTE report.

**SALUTE — Standard Spot Report:**

| Letter | Element | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Size | "Squad-sized element, approximately 6–8" |
| **A** | Activity | "Moving north on the road" / "Establishing fighting positions" |
| **L** | Location | "Grid 085 042" / "South treeline at the road junction" |
| **U** | Uniform | "Russian woodland camo, AKMs" |
| **T** | Time | "Time now" or specific time observed |
| **E** | Equipment | "One PKM, AT launcher, two trucks" |

**TAX — Weapon System Report (for crew-served and vehicle targets):**

| Letter | Element | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **T** | Type | What is the weapon system (e.g., PKM, RPG-7, BRDM) |
| **A** | Ammo | What ammunition type it is firing/carrying |
| **X** | Carriage | How it is mounted or transported (vehicle, static emplacement, man-packed) |
| **S** | Sights | Optics, night vision, thermal capability |

**Radio brevity:** You do not need to read the heading letters. Say: *"Contact south — squad-sized, moving toward the bridge, one PKM visible, grid 079 038, time now."* The structure is what matters, not the acronym recitation.

## ENABLER AND PREVENTER PLAY STYLES

Two fundamentally different approaches to combat power on a WCS server — both valuable, neither better than the other. Know which role you are playing and optimize for it.

### The Enabler

The Enabler expands the team's ability to project force by building and maintaining the infrastructure that makes action possible.

**Actions:**
- Supply runs that keep FOBs funded
- Spawning vehicles that give the team mobility
- Building radio arrays that extend coordination range
- Positioning a command truck that unlocks fire support
- Deploying a logistics truck at a forward position so the squad can spawn there

**Effect:** Players naturally flow toward well-supplied, well-equipped positions. An Enabler who keeps three forward FOBs funded and equipped attracts more players to the front than any amount of coordination requests. The team follows the supplies.

**Mindset:** You are not shooting enemies — you are determining whether your team *can* shoot enemies. Cap the supply flow, and the whole team's effectiveness collapses.

### The Preventer

The Preventer degrades the enemy's ability to project force by disrupting logistics and reinforcement rather than fighting at the objective.

**Actions:**
- Ambush on the road 1km behind the front — RPG on a jeep with 5 players inside eliminates five spawns simultaneously
- Route interdiction that forces the enemy to take longer, exposed routes
- Destruction of a supply truck restocking the frontline FOB
- Elimination of a radio relay station that severs the enemy's fire support coordination

**Effect:** The Preventer does not need to capture objectives. They reduce the enemy's capacity to reinforce the objective, which has the same result as capturing it — eventually the objective is undermanned and falls.

**Mindset:** Every kill behind enemy lines is worth two kills at the front. Players are resurge-able from the front; a destroyed supply truck takes minutes to replace and may leave the enemy FOB dry for the entire push.

> **Combined effect:** The Enabler and Preventer are most powerful operating simultaneously — your team floods the front with resources while the enemy's resources are being drained from behind. This is the two-part formula for controlling an entire match.

## SITUATIONAL AWARENESS — FIVE ELEMENTS

Situational awareness is not one thing. It is five parallel awareness tasks running simultaneously. A player who executes all five consistently makes better decisions than even an experienced player who only executes one.

### 1. Map Awareness

**Check your map at minimum every minute during active operations.**

- What objectives are being contested right now?
- Where are friendly forces? Where are the gaps in the line?
- Where was the last enemy contact? What direction were they moving?
- Mark enemy positions before engaging — map data benefits everyone, not just you

*A player who has not checked the map in five minutes is making decisions with five-minute-old information in a situation that may have changed completely.*

### 2. Sensory Awareness

Train yourself to process game audio as tactical data:

- **Footstep cadence** — running vs walking; direction; approaching or receding
- **Vehicle engine sound** — type and direction; proximity
- **Muzzle flash location** — at night, flash precedes sound; localize by flash before the crack
- **Shadow movement** — in buildings and at wood line edges, shadows move before the player is visible

What you hear and see peripherally is often more valuable than what you aim at.

### 3. Terrain Analysis

OCOKA in practice, continuously:

- Who holds the high ground right now, you or the enemy?
- Where are the choke points on the likely approach routes?
- What cover is available between your current position and the objective?
- Where would *you* set up an ambush on the route you are about to take? Go a different way.

Terrain does not change. Enemy behavior relative to terrain is predictable. Exploit this.

### 4. Enemy Intel Pattern Tracking

Build a picture of enemy behavior over time:

- What routes do they consistently use between supply depot and FOB?
- Where have engagements occurred repeatedly? Those positions are defended for a reason.
- Loot enemy radios (see below) for live comms
- After you die, note where you were killed from — that position exists because it is good ground

Pattern intelligence takes time to build but becomes enormously valuable by mid-game.

### 5. Predicting Enemy Actions

Think like the enemy commander:

- The enemy just lost their FOB at Novy — where are they likely to rebuild?
- Their supply depot was just hit — how long before they run dry at the front?
- You just eliminated their mortar crew — what is their indirect fire capability in the next 10 minutes?

Predicting what the enemy *will* do lets you position to counter it before it happens. Reactive play loses; predictive play wins.

## ENEMY RADIO LOOTING

When you neutralize an enemy soldier, check the body for a **radio**.

Picking up an enemy radio gives you access to the enemy communications net. You can monitor their voice comms and hear:
- Reinforcement plans and timings
- Movement orders ("push to Grid X")
- Medical calls that reveal how hard they were hit
- Command-level decisions about objective priorities

This is live, unfiltered enemy intelligence. Pass anything actionable to your Commander immediately.

> **Tactical note:** An enemy who discovers their net has been compromised may change frequencies. Treat looted enemy comms as a time-sensitive intelligence source — act on it fast.

# MOVEMENT AND FORMATIONS SOP
## Small Unit Movement for WCS Conflict

> **Doctrine basis:** ATP 3-21.8 Chapter 3 — Tactical Movement
> **Application:** All WCS servers, squad and fireteam movement

---

## WHY FORMATIONS MATTER

> *"Formations provide 360-degree security and allow units to give the weight of their firepower to the flanks or front in anticipation of enemy contact. They do not demand parade ground precision — units must retain the flexibility to vary their formations to the situation."*
> — ATP 3-21.8

In WCS Conflict, players default to moving in a cluster or a line because it is the path of least resistance. This is why those squads die to a single MG or a single grenade.

Formations solve three problems simultaneously:
- They spread the squad so one weapon cannot hit everyone
- They ensure every direction is covered by someone
- They put the squad in the right shape to respond to contact without giving additional orders

A squad that knows its formation and spacing can react to contact, execute a bounding withdrawal, or transition to assault with nothing more than a two-word order from the squad leader.

---

## SPACING — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE RULE

**Maintain 10–15 meters between players at all times during movement.**

This is the single most important thing in this document. Everything else is refinement.

A frag grenade has a lethal radius of roughly 5 meters. A burst of automatic fire covers a 2-meter swath. Players clustered together amplify the effect of every enemy weapon. Players at proper spacing mean the enemy must engage each player separately.

Spacing also breaks up visual signatures. A line of eight players 2 meters apart looks like a single target. Eight players at 15-meter intervals look like multiple, dispersed threats — much harder to engage effectively.

## MOVEMENT TECHNIQUES

ATP 3-21.8 defines three movement techniques, each representing a different balance between speed and security. Choose based on the threat level.

### Traveling

**Use when:** Enemy contact is not likely. You need to move fast.
**How:** Full squad moves together, normal spacing, no element providing overwatch.
**Risk:** Vulnerable to ambush. No overwatch means no immediate response.
**In WCS:** Moving from main base to a friendly-held FOB deep in rear areas.

### Traveling Overwatch

**Use when:** Enemy contact is possible but not expected.
**How:** Lead element moves ahead by 50–100 meters. Trail element follows, maintaining observation of the lead. Trail element can provide immediate fire if lead makes contact.
**In WCS:** Moving from a FOB toward a contested objective area. Standard movement through uncertain terrain.

### Bounding Overwatch

**Use when:** Enemy contact is likely or expected. Crossing open ground. Moving through known threat areas.
**How:** One element establishes overwatch (weapons up, covering the route). The other bounds to the next covered position. Alternate.
**In WCS:** Any movement across open ground, approaching an objective, or moving through an area where enemy was recently seen.

**Bounding overwatch is the default technique whenever you are within visual range of a contested objective.**

## MOVEMENT FORMATIONS

### The Wedge — Standard Patrol Formation

```
              ● POINT
            /   \
          ●       ●
        /           \
      ●               ●
              ●
            REAR
```

**Purpose:** The primary formation for movement in open terrain with moderate threat. Provides observation and firepower to the front and both flanks.

**How it works:**
- Point man leads and sets the pace. Covers the front.
- Left and right elements cover their respective flanks.
- Rear element covers behind.
- Squad leader typically positions center-rear for visibility and control.

**Transitions:** The wedge naturally transitions to online (assault), to a single file (dense terrain), or to bounding overwatch (threat contact) with minimal repositioning.

**WCS application:** Default formation from a FOB outward toward any objective. Maintain visual contact between elements — close enough to see each other, far enough to not all die to one grenade.

---

### Column — Movement Along Linear Features

```
● POINT
|
● 2ND
|
● 3RD
|
● REAR
```

**Purpose:** Fastest movement formation. Used along roads, through forests, or any terrain that forces linear movement.

**Critical weakness:** Column presents a linear target to flanking fire. An MG position at 90 degrees to a column in the open can engage every player in sequence.

**Rules for column:**
- Never use on open ground if a flank can be observed by the enemy
- Always keep spacing — bunch-ups in column are ambush magnets
- The front player sets the pace and the direction; the rest follow

**WCS application:** Moving along roads between bases in friendly-controlled areas. Moving through dense forest where the wedge is not practical. Always transition out of column when approaching contested areas.

---

### Line — Assault Formation

```
● — ● — ● — ● — ●
```

**Purpose:** The assault formation. All players advance simultaneously toward a position, maximizing firepower to the front.

**Use only when:**
- You have positive identification of the enemy position
- Suppressive fire has been established on that position
- You are ready to physically assault through and clear it

**Not a movement formation.** Do not patrol in line — it exposes the flanks completely.

**WCS application:** Final 50–100 meter assault on an enemy position after support by fire has been established. Transition from bounding overwatch or wedge when you are ready to go.

---

### File — Dense Terrain / Corridor Movement

```
● POINT
↓
● 2ND
↓
● 3RD
↓
● REAR
```

**Purpose:** Single file when terrain forces it — narrow paths, dense forest, building corridors.

**Difference from column:** File is used when there is literally no room for any other formation. Column is a choice; file is a constraint.

**Rule:** As soon as terrain permits spreading out, get back into wedge or traveling overwatch.

## TRANSITION SIGNALS

Squad leaders should use simple calls to transition formations:

| Call | Formation |
|---|---|
| "Wedge up" | Form the wedge, squad leader center |
| "Online" | Form the assault line — contact imminent |
| "Column" / "File" | Linear movement |
| "Bound" | Bounding overwatch — one element holds, one moves |
| "Rally at [landmark]" | Break contact, move to specified point |

## CROSSING OPEN GROUND

This is where squads die in WCS. Open ground = exposure. There is no clever workaround — open ground is dangerous and must be crossed correctly or avoided entirely.

**Before crossing:**
1. Observe the far side for threats. Use LRF to check likely positions.
2. Identify cover on the far side — where you will go when you get there.
3. Identify cover on the near side — where your overwatch element will set up.

**Crossing:**
1. One element establishes overwatch — weapons up, covering the crossing route and the far side.
2. Second element crosses at the run, moving directly to the identified cover position.
3. Second element sets overwatch. First element crosses.

**Rules:**
- Never walk across open ground if you can run
- Go prone behind cover before setting overwatch — do not stand in the open
- If you take fire while crossing, immediately go prone and low-crawl to the nearest depression — do not run upright under fire

## PATROL BASE AND HALTS

When the squad stops for any reason — to observe, to treat a casualty, to get bearings — it does not cluster.

**Short halt:** All elements drop to a knee or prone. Maintain formation. Cover assigned sectors. No talking. Pass information by tap or hand signal.

**Longer halt / Patrol base:** Squad forms a small perimeter with all weapons outward. No position faces the same direction as the adjacent position. Squad leader at center. One player on radio.

**Security during halts is not optional.** The halt is when the squad is most vulnerable. A squad that clusters around the squad leader during a halt will be killed by a single grenade.

---

## NOISE AND LIGHT DISCIPLINE

During patrol, the squad should be as quiet as movement in a game engine allows:

- Communicate by radio at minimal necessary volume
- Do not fire unless necessary — gunshots mark your position
- At night, IR lasers and NVGs only — visible light (flashlights) are seen by anyone looking in your direction
- Engine noise from vehicles carries — in a patrol, foot movement is quieter than vehicle movement

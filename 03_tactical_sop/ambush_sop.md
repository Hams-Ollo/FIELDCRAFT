# AMBUSH SOP
## Deliberate Ambush Procedures for WCS Conflict

> **Doctrine basis:** FM 7-8 Chapter 3 — Patrolling; Ambush Operations
> **Application:** WCS servers — squad-level point ambush, vehicle interdiction, supply route denial

---

## AMBUSH DEFINED

> *"An ambush is a surprise attack by fire from a concealed position on a moving or temporarily halted enemy."* — FM 7-8

An ambush is a **preemptive, deliberate action.** It requires planning, positioning, patience, and disciplined execution. It is not a firefight that happens to start from concealment — it is a scripted destruction sequence with assigned roles, triggers, and withdrawal procedures.

In WCS Conflict, the ambush is the most effective way for a small element to destroy a larger force, eliminate vehicle patrols, and interdict supply convoys.

---

## AMBUSH TYPES

### Point Ambush — Single Kill Zone

A single element attacks a single target from one or more positions along a route. The majority of WCS ambushes are point ambushes — one squad, one kill zone.

**Components:**
- **Assault element** — fires into and clears the kill zone
- **Support element** — provides flanking or enfilading fire; prevents escape from one end
- **Security element** — covers both flanks and the enemy's likely approach and withdrawal routes

### Area Ambush — Multiple Kill Zones

A principal ambush site with two or more subsidiary sites covering likely withdrawal or reinforcement routes. Used at platoon level when the enemy is being funneled through a known choke point.

**WCS use:** One element on the primary road; additional elements covering side routes to neutralize survivors or reinforcements.

---

## AMBUSH FORMATIONS

| Formation | Layout | Best use |
|---|---|---|
| **Linear** | Entire element parallel to the enemy route | Close terrain (forest, ditches); weapons fire along the axis of march |
| **L-shaped** | Main element along route; flank element perpendicular at one end of kill zone | Trail bends, road junctions; delivers flanking and enfilading fire simultaneously |
| **Area** | Multiple point ambushes on a principal site plus outlying sites | Enemy funneled to single trail or stream; platoon minimum required |
| **Antiarmor** | AT/MAW team in primary kill position; security covers flanks and withdrawal routes | Vehicle convoys; kill first and last vehicle to close the box |

### The L-Shaped Ambush — Recommended Default

The L-shaped ambush is the most effective formation for ambushing column formations in WCS because it:
- Prevents the enemy from returning effective fire in a single direction
- Catches the front of the column in crossfire from two axes
- Uses the short leg to block withdrawal from one end of the kill zone

```
ENEMY ROUTE →  → → → → [KILL ZONE] → → →
                         ↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑↑
Long Leg: ●●●●●●●●●●  (parallel to route)
                               ●
                               ●  Short Leg
                               ●  (perpendicular,
                               ●   at FAR end of
                               ●   kill zone)
```

**Short leg fires down the length of the column** (enfilading fire — the most lethal fire geometry because every round can hit a target in the line). **Long leg fires into the flank.** The combined effect is an L-shaped field of fire with no safe direction for the enemy.

## VEHICLE AMBUSH — ANTIARMOR SEQUENCING

Vehicle ambushes in WCS Conflict require specific target sequencing. Do not engage vehicles in the order they appear — engage them in the order that traps the convoy.

**Target sequence:**
1. **Kill the LAST vehicle first** — blocks the convoy from reversing out of the kill zone
2. **Kill the FIRST vehicle second** — prevents forward escape
3. **Engage remaining vehicles** in the kill zone from most dangerous to least

**Why this order:** A vehicle column that loses its lead vehicle will attempt to reverse. A column that loses its trail vehicle cannot reverse without running over wreckage or moving through fire. Losing both creates a closed kill box from which no vehicle can exit without crossing your fires.

**Weapon assignment — brief before initiation:**
- AT launcher (Carl Gustav, RPG, LAW) or MG with AP ammunition kills vehicles
- Riflemen engage dismounts that exit vehicles
- Do not use rifle fire against armored vehicles — it wastes ammunition and your position

**Initiation discipline:** The ambush is initiated by ONE designated weapon — typically the AT launcher on the lead vehicle or the MG on the trail vehicle. No one fires until the initiation weapon fires. Premature fire by any element lets the convoy react before the kill zone is full.

## AMBUSH PLANNING AND PREPARATION

### Route Reconnaissance

Before occupying the ambush site:
- Confirm the enemy uses this route — direct observation or recon element reporting
- Identify the actual kill zone: flat ground, limited cover for the enemy, clear fields of fire for you
- Identify your withdrawal route — you need to leave quickly after the action

### Site Selection Criteria

- **Fields of fire** — Can all weapons cover the kill zone without obstruction? No dead ground inside the kill zone.
- **Cover and concealment** — Are your positions behind terrain that prevents skylining? Can the enemy spot you from the route?
- **Command and control** — Can the SL see all elements, or at least maintain radio contact?
- **Covered withdrawal** — Is there continuous terrain cover between the ambush site and the ORP?

### Position Assignment Brief

Before movement to the ambush site, brief all players:
- Kill zone boundaries (exact start and end markers)
- Initiation weapon and trigger condition *(example: "AT launcher fires when the lead vehicle reaches the stone wall")*
- Target assignments (who engages which vehicle or element)
- Withdrawal signal and route
- ORP location and linkup signal

---

## OCCUPATION AND FINAL PREPARATION

### Movement to Ambush Site

Move to the **ORP (Objective Rally Point)** — a covered position short of the ambush site that is not visible from the enemy route. From the ORP, the squad leader conducts a leader's reconnaissance to confirm the site and assign exact positions.

**Occupation sequence:**
1. Security element in place first — covers flanks while the main element occupies
2. Support element in place second
3. Assault element last
4. Squad leader confirms all positions are set via radio

### CRITICAL: Weapons Off Safe Before the Enemy Arrives

**All weapons must come off safe BEFORE the enemy enters the kill zone.**

The mechanical click of a selector switch — SAFE to FIRE — will compromise your position if the enemy is near. Take your weapon off safe when you are in your final position, not at the last second. Confirm to adjacent team members with a hand signal, not a radio transmission.

### Trigger and Execution Confirmation

Once all elements are in position, the SL verifies:
- All elements confirm position *(one transmission per element — short)*
- Confirm the trigger condition: *"Initiation on AT launcher when lead vehicle reaches the burning tree"*
- All elements acknowledge
- **Radio silence until initiation**

---

## ACTIONS IN THE KILL ZONE

### Initiation

- Initiation weapon fires on the designated trigger
- All other weapons open fire immediately — there is no second trigger
- MG opens with a mad minute — maximum shock effect in the first 10 seconds
- AT launcher engages vehicles in the designated sequence

### Fire Control

- All fire stays within the kill zone boundaries
- Do not pursue targets that have exited the kill zone — this exposes your flanks and your position
- Security elements report any enemy flanking movement immediately

### Cease Fire

The SL calls cease fire when:
- All enemy in the kill zone are neutralized, OR
- SL assesses the kill zone is no longer receiving effective enemy fire

**"Cease fire" is called by the SL only.** Individual players stop firing when ordered — not when they run low on ammo or think the fight is over.

## ASSAULT AND SEARCH

### Decision to Assault

Assault through the kill zone only when:
- All resistance has stopped, OR
- The SL assesses the kill zone is no longer covered by effective enemy fire

**Do not assault a kill zone that is still under enemy fire.** Maintain suppression until clear, then assault.

### Assault Execution

- Support element continues to cover flanks throughout
- Assault element moves through the kill zone in a line, clearing each position
- Call "Clear!" for each section
- Move quickly — you are exposed during the search phase

### Two-Man Search Technique (FM 7-8)

**Purpose:** Search enemy dead for intelligence while protecting against booby traps.

| Position | Player | Actions |
|---|---|---|
| **Guard** | Player 1 | Stands at the enemy's head, weapon oriented toward any threat direction. Does NOT search. |
| **Searcher** | Player 2 | Starts at head, works systematically to feet. |

**Search sequence:**
1. If face-down: roll the body **toward the searcher's own body** — not away. This protects against a grenade placed under the body detonating toward the searcher.
2. **Head and neck:** rank insignia, dog tags, lanyards, cards
3. **Chest and pockets:** documents, maps, notebooks, crypto cards, SOI
4. **Waistband and rear pockets:** additional documents, pistol
5. **Legs and ankles:** holdout weapons, additional items
6. **Pack/ruck:** radio equipment, maps, frequency lists, additional documents

**Items to take:** Maps, documents with writing, radio frequency cards, SOI cards. Do not take personal items — this is intelligence collection.

## WITHDRAWAL

Withdrawal from the ambush site is as important as the initiation. Assume you have 5–10 minutes before enemy reinforcement arrives after a vehicle ambush — less if the contact was within radio range of a large enemy force.

### Withdrawal Sequence

1. **SL calls withdrawal** when kill zone is searched and security reports clean flanks
2. **Assault element withdraws first** — moves directly to ORP
3. **Support element withdraws second** — covers assault element withdrawal
4. **Security element withdraws last** — falls back through the ORP
5. All elements consolidate at ORP; SL verifies headcount before moving to the next position

### Withdrawal Route Rules

- **Never withdraw on the same route used to occupy the site** — if the enemy tracked your approach, that route is compromised
- Move 90–180 degrees from the ambush site axis before turning toward friendly lines
- Use terrain (ridgelines, treelines, dead ground) to mask the withdrawal from the kill zone

## WCS-SPECIFIC: SUPPLY ROUTE INTERDICTION

In WCS Conflict, the primary high-value ambush target is the enemy **supply convoy** — logistics vehicles moving between FOBs and supply points. Supply convoy kills compound: they deny resources, reduce enemy operational tempo, and generate intelligence on logistics patterns.

### Supply Route Reconnaissance

Before planning a supply interdiction:
1. Observe the route for at least one resupply cycle to confirm vehicle types and timing
2. Identify vehicle composition — unarmored trucks vs armored vehicles require different weapon loads
3. Identify escort element size — have they added security after previous ambushes on this route?
4. Identify alternate routes — will they change routes after the first successful hit?

### Preferred Site Characteristics in WCS

- **Narrow road sections** — limits vehicle maneuver off-road into cover
- **Route curves** — lead and trail vehicles cannot see each other; crossfire is easier to establish
- **Elevation advantage on at least one side** — plunging fire against vehicle roof is more effective than flat-angle fire against the hull
- **Concealed withdrawal route** — dense terrain or a ridgeline on your exfil axis

### Timing Considerations

A supply truck with a known timing pattern can be ambushed on its second or third pass once you have confirmed the route. The first observation is reconnaissance. The second observation sets the pattern. The third is the trigger.

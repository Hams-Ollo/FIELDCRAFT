# 📋 MISSION PLANNING FRAMEWORK
## Pre-Session Briefing Template for Zen Solutions

> **Doctrine basis:** US Army 5-Paragraph Operations Order (OPORD) — adapted for WCS Conflict
> **Use:** Before any coordinated Zen Solutions session. Takes 3–5 minutes. Prevents 30 minutes of confusion.

---

## 💬 WHY BRIEF AT ALL

The 5-paragraph OPORD is the US Army's standard briefing format. It ensures everyone going into an operation understands the same picture: what is happening, what we are doing, how we are doing it, who handles what, and how we communicate.

In a WCS session, you do not need a 45-minute formal briefing. You need 3–5 minutes on voice before the first player spawns in. That brief time eliminates the four most common failures of uncoordinated squads:

- Players pushing different objectives with no shared priority
- Nobody running logistics because everyone assumed someone else was
- No designated medic because the role was never assigned
- Squad leader calling fire missions nobody can hear because frequencies were never set

The format below is stripped to the minimum that actually matters in a WCS match. It follows the 5-paragraph structure but uses plain language.

---

## 📝 THE FIVE PARAGRAPHS — WCS ADAPTATION

### 1. 🌍 SITUATION

**What is happening on the map right now.**

Cover:
- **Enemy:** What Russia is doing or likely to do based on the map. Where their supply routes are. Whether they have established forward FOBs. What their current objective holds are.
- **Friendly:** What NATO's current situation is — which objectives are held, what infrastructure exists, what our supply levels look like. Other squads and what they are doing if known.

**Target length:** 3–4 sentences. Do not turn this into a lecture. Hit the key points.

*Example: "Russia has main base northeast and currently holds Kansas, Iowa, and Michigan. They appear to have a logistics chain running through the central corridor. NATO holds our main base and has a FOB at Staroye. Supply levels at the forward FOB are moderate."*

---

### 2. 🎯 MISSION

**One sentence. What we are doing and why.**

The mission statement has two parts: the task (what) and the purpose (why). Both matter. Players who know why they are doing something make better decisions when the plan changes.

**Format:** *"[Unit] will [task] in order to [purpose]."*

*Examples:*
- *"Zen Solutions will establish fire support at Pucker Point in order to disrupt Russia's ability to reinforce and resupply their western objectives."*
- *"Zen Solutions will assault and hold Mogilevka in order to cut Russia's central supply corridor and open the western axis for the main NATO push."*
- *"Blue Falcon will establish a forward OP overlooking Chernogorsk in order to feed fire missions on Russia's primary logistics chain."*

---

### 3. ⚙️ EXECUTION

**How we are doing it. This is the bulk of the brief.**

Cover:
- **Concept of the operation:** The big picture of how the mission will unfold. Phase 1, Phase 2, etc. if applicable.
- **Tasks to elements:** What is each person or sub-element doing specifically?
- **Coordinating instructions:** Key timing, triggers, control measures. When do we push? What is the trigger to shift from logistics to assault? What happens if the FOB is destroyed?

*Example:*
*"Concept: We will drive an MCU to Pucker Point, establish the COP, and begin fire missions on Mogilevka while the main NATO element pushes from the east. Blue Falcon leads the LRRP to the OP position while I build out the COP.*

*Tasks: [Player A] drives the MCU and serves as mortar crew. [Player B] and [Player C] are Blue Falcon — LRRP to OP grid 087 044, establish overwatch on Mogilevka, call fire missions. [Player D] runs logistics from main base to Pucker Point.*

*Coordinating: We do not fire until the COP is concealed and the mortar pits are up. Blue Falcon does not engage until at least two fire missions are complete. If the MCU is destroyed, we fall back to grid 082 038 and reassess."*

---

### 4. 🔧 SERVICE AND SUPPORT

**Logistics, medical, and equipment.**

Cover:
- **Supplies:** Who is running logistics? What is the supply plan?
- **Medical:** Who is the designated medic? What is the MEDEVAC plan?
- **Equipment:** Any specific vehicles, special kit, or equipment requirements?

*Example: "[Player D] handles logistics. Supply priority is mortar pit stocks first, then armory. If we need a medic, [Player B] has the largest IFAK and can break off the LRRP to treat. Everyone has a zero-cost loadout saved before we depart."*

---

### 5. 📹 COMMAND AND SIGNAL

**Who is in charge, how do we communicate.**

Cover:
- **Command:** Who is squad leader? Who is second in command if the SL goes down?
- **Radio frequencies:** Long-range (platoon net) frequency. Squad net frequency. Any special frequencies for fire missions.
- **Signals:** Any brevity codes specific to this session. Rally point if separated.

*Example: "I am SL. [Player A] is 2IC if I go down. Squad net on [frequency]. Blue Falcon will call fire missions on long-range radio — mortar crew monitor that frequency throughout. Rally point at main base if Pucker Point is overrun. Use 'Winchester' to flag ammo status, 'Litter' for casualty, 'OP compromised' if Blue Falcon is blown."*

---

## ⏱️ THE ONE-MINUTE BRIEF

When there is no time for a full brief — server just hit 32v32 and it is live — use this compressed format:

```
SITUATION:   "Russia holds [X]. We need [Y]."

MISSION:     "We will [task] in order to [purpose]."

EXECUTION:   "[Player A] does this. [Player B] does that. 
              We push when [trigger]."

LOGISTICS:   "[Player] handles supply. [Player] is medic."

COMMS:       "SL is [name]. Squad net [freq]. Rally at [point]."
```

This takes under 60 seconds and ensures everyone deploys with a shared picture.

---

## 🔄 POST-MISSION DEBRIEF

After a session, a 5-minute debrief captures lessons before they are forgotten. Three questions:

1. **What worked?** What went well that we want to repeat.
2. **What did not work?** What failed and why.
3. **What do we change?** One or two specific adjustments for next session.

Keep it positive and forward-looking. The goal is to get better, not to assign blame.

Debrief notes can be added to the map intelligence file for the map played — operational lessons from real sessions are the most valuable content in any tactical library.

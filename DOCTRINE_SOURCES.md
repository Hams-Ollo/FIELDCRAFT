## WHY WE USE REAL DOCTRINE

Real military doctrine is not arbitrary. It represents centuries of warfare distilled into tested principles — lessons paid for in blood that describe why certain approaches work and others get people killed. When we apply these principles to Arma Reforger, we are not LARPing. We are using the most battle-validated tactical framework available and translating it into a game that was designed to simulate these exact scenarios.

The game engine changes. The map changes. The weapons change. The principles do not.

## PRIMARY SOURCES

### ATP 3-21.8 — Infantry Platoon and Squad
**Replaces:** FM 3-21.8 (March 2007), ATTP 3-21.71, ATTP 3-21.9
**Published:** April 2016 (current edition)
**Available at:** US Army Publishing Directorate — armypubs.army.mil
**Also available:** Fort Moore Infantry School — moore.army.mil/infantry/doctrinesupplement/ATP3-21.8

**What it covers:**
The doctrinal framework for how infantry rifle platoons and squads fight. This is the foundational small-unit tactics manual covering movement formations, fire and movement principles, tactical tasks, offensive and defensive operations, and immediate action drills.

**Key principles we use from this source:**
- Fire and movement as the fundamental tactic: fire without movement is indecisive; exposed movement without fire is potentially disastrous
- Movement formations: wedge, column, line, file, and echelon — their purposes and when to use each
- Movement techniques: traveling, traveling overwatch, and bounding overwatch
- Immediate action drills: react to contact, react to ambush, break contact
- Bounding overwatch mechanics: bounds, base of fire, assault element
- Ambush categories: point, area, and specific forms including the L-shape
- Defense: sector sketches, interlocking fields of fire, obstacle integration

**How we adapted it:**
Real bounding overwatch accounts for terrain analysis, METT-TC, and physical stamina. In the game, we simplify to the core mechanic: one element provides suppressive fire while another moves. The principle is identical. The execution is adapted to Arma's mechanics and map scale.

### FM 6-30 / ATP 3-09.30 — Observed Fire / Fire Support
**FM 6-30:** Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures for Observed Fire (legacy, widely referenced)
**ATP 3-09.30:** Techniques for Observed Fire (current edition)
**Available at:** globalsecurity.org/military/library/policy/army/fm/6-30/ (FM 6-30)
**Army pubs:** armypubs.army.mil (ATP 3-09.30)

**What it covers:**
The complete procedures for calling and adjusting indirect fire — mortars, artillery, naval gunfire, and close air support. Covers the six-element call for fire, adjustment procedures, observer duties, observation post selection, and target reference point methodology.

**Key principles we use from this source:**
- The six-element call for fire (identification and warning order, target location, target description, method of engagement, method of fire and control) — our CFF format follows this structure exactly
- Observer self-location: the observer must accurately determine their own position before they can accurately locate targets
- Observation post selection criteria: observation over the target, concealment from the target, multiple egress routes
- Target reference points (TRPs): pre-registered known points that allow rapid fire missions without recalculating — the foundation of our Pucker Point mortar table doctrine
- Adjustment of fire: bracketing, shift from a known point, creeping — the adjustment calls in our CFF SOP are taken directly from FM 6-30
- Danger Close criteria and procedures
- The FIST (Fire Support Team) concept: fire support personnel embedded with maneuver elements, acting as the eyes of indirect fire assets

**How we adapted it:**
Real observed fire uses six-digit or eight-digit grids, polar plots, and shift-from-known-point calculations. In the game, we use the map's coordinate grid directly. The call format is simplified from the full FM 6-30 format to what is executable on a radio under fire, but every element is present and the sequence is identical.

## SUPPORTING DOCTRINE CONCEPTS

These concepts from doctrine appear throughout the library even when not explicitly cited:

**METT-TC** (Mission, Enemy, Terrain and weather, Troops available, Time available, Civil considerations)
The standard military analysis framework for any tactical decision. When this library discusses how to approach an objective or position a unit, METT-TC is the analytical framework underneath. In the game context: Mission = current objective, Enemy = what Russia is doing, Terrain = the map, Troops = what your squad has, Time = where you are in the match.

**OCOKA** (Observation and fields of fire, Cover and concealment, Obstacles, Key terrain, Avenues of approach)
The terrain analysis framework. Used when selecting OP positions, FOB locations, ambush positions, and defensive emplacements. Every placement decision in this library — from where to put a mortar pit to where to position an MG nest — applies OCOKA.

**5-Paragraph OPORD** (Situation, Mission, Execution, Service and Support, Command and Signal)
The standard military briefing format. Our Mission Planning Framework adapts this for pre-session briefings in Arma. Even a two-minute voice brief using this structure produces dramatically better coordination than no briefing at all.

**SALUTE Report** (Size, Activity, Location, Unit, Time, Equipment)
The standard format for contact and intelligence reports. When Blue Falcon observes an enemy position and calls it to the SL, this is the format — adapted to our brevity codes.

## IMPORTANT NOTE ON ADAPTATION

This library does not reproduce text from these field manuals. It uses the principles, adapts them to game mechanics, and attributes them appropriately. Players who want to go deeper into real doctrine are encouraged to read the source materials — they are publicly available, well-written, and genuinely interesting.

The goal is not to teach soldiers how to fight real wars. The goal is to use the best-tested tactical framework available to help Arma players understand *why* coordinated squads win, and give them the vocabulary and procedures to do it themselves.

## OTHER REFERENCES

**Sun Tzu — The Art of War**
Several passages from Sun Tzu appear throughout this library as framing for larger strategic concepts — particularly on intelligence asymmetry, logistics, and momentum. These are not tactical references but philosophical ones, and they resonate in the WCS context because Sun Tzu's core insight — that battles are won before they are fought, through preparation, positioning, and intelligence — maps directly onto how WCS matches are actually won.
